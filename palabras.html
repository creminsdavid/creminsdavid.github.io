Las palabras que aprendí (The Words That I Have Learned)

913 words. 5 months. 154 days. Nearly 6 per day, although sometimes there were as few as just 1 (apoderar, 27th of October) or as many as 18, morsels picked out from conversations, advertisements in the subway, classes, books, songs, from mere curiosity, a thousand different ways. All these capsulized memories, more vivid than any journal or blog, taking up every available line of my little blue notebook. This tattered, handmade dictionary chronicles it all, from my arrival in Buenos Aires on the brisk winter morning of the 18th of July when I learned morder and correa as I walked Banda for the first time until the ends of hot December when I picked up on the word represa from a museum exhibit sign. In between, the pages of scrawled black ink speak to me, not a week removed from my time in Argentina, of months and friends and places forever close to me and already far away.

Mandarina and desabrigado are for when I could barely understand the cashier at the frutería and foolhardily thought I could explore the city in just a t-shirt. Bárbaro, asado and buena onda call up dozens of nights amongst the porteños and my fellow exchange students, with too much Quilmes and not enough Malbec. I recall the ganas I had to find some peanut butter bien crujiente in Barrio Chino, my first bitter sip from the bombilla, and trying to understand Vicky, the daughter of our AirBNB hosts in Córdoba, talking about her favorite peluche. Hundreds of words, such as matiz from the 18th of August or índole from the 12th of September, I have no recollection where they came from, while others could never be mistaken (ojo che not to mix up conservante y preservativo, ¿viste?).

Glosario (Glossary)

apoderar (to authorize) morder (to bite) correa (leash)
