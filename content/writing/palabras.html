Las palabras que aprendí (The Words That I Learned)

913 words. 5 months. 154 days. Nearly 6 per day, although sometimes there were as few as just 1 (apoderar, 27th of October) or as many as 18, morsels picked out from conversations, advertisements in the subway, classes, books, songs, from mere curiosity, a thousand different ways. All these capsulized memories, more vivid than any journal or blog, taking up every available line of my little blue notebook. This tattered, handmade dictionary chronicles it all, from my arrival in Buenos Aires on the brisk winter morning of the 18th of July when I learned morder and correa as I walked Banda for the first time until the ends of hot December when I picked up on the word represa from a museum exhibit sign. In between, the pages of scrawled black ink speak to me, not a week removed from my time in Argentina, of months and friends and places forever close to me and already far away.

Mandarina and desabrigado are for when I could barely understand the cashier at the frutería and foolhardily thought I could explore the city in just a t-shirt. Bárbaro, asado and buena onda call up dozens of nights amongst the porteños and my fellow exchange students, with too much Quilmes and not enough Malbec. I recall the ganas I had to find some peanut butter bien crujiente in Barrio Chino, my first bitter sip from the bombilla, and trying to understand Vicky, the daughter of our AirBNB hosts in Córdoba, talking about her favorite peluche. Hundreds of words, such as matiz from the 18th of August or índole from the 12th of September, I have no recollection where they came from, while others could never be mistaken (ojo che not to mix up conservante y preservativo, ¿viste?).

Until you’re forced to get by on a regular basis, in the home and in the school, of a foreign country, it’s difficult to realize how many words high school vocabulary books can never cover, and so I learned tomacorriente and carilla, baúl and bolsillo. But then again, when it comes to valija, frutilla, palta, anana, or zapatilla, those textbooks would not have helped in Argentina anyway.

Some entries in the blue dictionary are filled with sadness and pain: pésame on the 23rd of September when my Nana passed away, followed by ataúd after several days of silence while I was in Dublin. On the 9th of November, the day following the election, there are only two words written: entumecido and facho. However, these darker moments are swamped, at least in quantity, by memories of tintineo in Mendoza or sambayón in the milonga.

Reading through texts for my philosophy and history classes, and Borges in my ample free time, offered more than enough terms for my consumption, ranging from libre albedrío to aleatorio. However, the best education came through just paying attention to the richness of daily life, to the meanings I previously did not appreciate in comprometer or the basic need to refer to a muelle just off the adoquines while on the beaches of Uruguay.

Some words are surprising in their utility: destornillador came in handy both in fixing a plumbing issue and in a bar, meanwhile maracuyá appeared in most every heladería and feria, and an afternoon concert in the Teatro Colón taught us that araña can also mean chandelier.

While I would like to claim that I learned many great words such as fulano from my internship at Fulbright, the truth is I picked up more from Netflix and podcasts, ranging from pepino to preso, cachorro to vitrina. I am proud to say, though, that much of my most prized vocabulary came from the national religion (¡vamos River!): partidario, garras, bronca, forro, yuta…it is a beautiful language.

There are too many words concerning food and drink to even touch on here, but from the masas rellenas con fugazzeta of Pizzería Guerrín to a famous tira cooked over the brasas in the countryside of the province with luciérnagas flickering above, there are already enough reasons to return south soon.

I could go on, stories about seeing my first vicuña in the mountains of Jujuy or exploring the niebla of Bariloche with my girlfriend or the cataratas of Iguazú with my family, but I would risk not including dozens of other cherished memories with no specific word attached. Instead, I will end with a thanks to my host family, Rocío and Santi in beautiful Muñiz in Almagro, and to the coordinators and teachers from Middlebury who made these incredible months possible, but most of all because these are the people who taught me lunfardo: my utility with copado, pibe, chorro, guita, quilombo, posta, piola, bajón, capo, possibly my favorite, to be used liberally in Recoleta and Palermo: cheto, is all due to them.

These words I have written down, and surely the hundreds others I learned but never had occasion to, do not tell the story of what these last few months away from my home and loved ones has meant for me, but they do a pretty damn good job. I will never claim to be fluent in castellano, but the opportunity to have become this much closer to the language, the country, and the continent means literally the world to me - it is that much more mine now.
